A Retrospect

What I've Learned Throughout my University Journey

Scroll Down
01 Learning 1

Every Expert Was Once A Beginner

In a program as rigorous and reputable as UWaterloo's Computer Science, it is easy to feel unworthy and incompotent in face of difficult content and well-accomplished peers. However, through experience I've learned that, not only is humility a necessity to improve, but it also yields the best results. Use your fears and nerves to motivate yourself to put extra effort into learning (the right way), and you'll find yourself better off in the long run. After all, as Epictetus said, "It is impossible for one to learn what he thinks he already knows.”

02 Learning 2

Take Care of your Body

Academic success at the expense of health and well-being is no success at all. While it may seem tempting for short-term rewards, sacrificing well-being always has a cascading downwards effect. Exercise and a healthy diet have long been known to enhance academic performance and overall mood, and will increase the payoff of your degree's hard-work in the future.

03 Learning 3

Minimize Distractions

Working on stressful and overwhelming tasks on your computer every day makes it extremely difficult to build the "discipline" that other successful students have. Thus, it's best to be proactive; leave your apartment, block distracting sites, turn off your phone... these simple actions can save a lot of regret and struggle, and will maximize your speed and efficiency.

04 Learning 4

Make Time for Hobbies

The word make here is essential. UW CS wil eat up every second of time you give it. Unless coding is your hobby and passion, devoting every second of the day to your studies will inevitably lead to burn out and a strong dissatisfaction with your life. Instead, to maintain strong mental health in the long-term, devote time, at least 30 minutes a day, to something you find fun or meaningful. Again, your program and marks are not more valuable than your own well-being.

Read more
05 Learning 5

Adopt a Growth-based Mindset

Surrounded by some of the smartest and most well-accomplished people I've ever met, I constantly felt inadequate and unhappy duirng my first two years in the program. However, rather than comparison to others, whether it be status, knowledge, or marks, the only worthy subject for comparison is yourself. I have felt far more at ease and seen far greater improvements after adopting a growth-based mindset in which my goals are not qualitative or external; rather, they lie completely within myself and my learning.
